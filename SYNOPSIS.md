# Synopsis — The Woman in My Phone
### by A. Rathna
**Literary Fiction | 57,000 words | Complete**

---

ARI VELMURUGAN is a thirty-two-year-old Tamil American tech professional in Seattle with a TikTok following that fills the silence his marriage can't. His wife PRIYA exists three feet away in the same bed and a thousand miles away in every way that matters. They haven't touched in years. They don't fight. They don't anything.

In July 2018, a woman named MEERA starts winning Ari's Live song games every night. She knows every A.R. Rahman melody. She's funny, sharp, and achingly present in a way no one in his physical life is. Their first DM becomes a four-hour video call. Within ten days, they've crossed every boundary two married strangers can cross through a screen — she orgasms from his voice alone, and both of them know this is no longer a game.

What Ari doesn't know yet: Meera's husband VIKRAM rapes her. The phone isn't her escape. It's her only room.

Their lives fall into a rhythm of 137-hour call months and parallel cages — his silent, hers violent. Ari flies to India. Three days in a hotel room where they condense a lifetime into seventy-two hours. On the last morning, they have sex without protection for the first time. He watches from a taxi as she walks back toward the man who hurts her.

Meera discovers she's pregnant. "It could be yours," she tells Ari, and for three years he lives inside that *could*. It's not his — the timeline never worked — but the ambiguity becomes its own kind of parenthood: loving a child who might carry your DNA across an ocean you can't cross.

Then Meera goes silent. Thirteen months. No calls, no texts, no explanation. Ari unravels — sitting in parking lots staring at her contact photo, Googling "how long until someone is legally dead," rage-crying to Tamil songs at 2 AM. From Meera's side, we learn the silence wasn't abandonment. She was holding her newborn daughter at 3 AM, staring at Ari's name in her phone, choosing motherhood over the only man who ever made her feel alive. Trying to make her marriage work. Failing.

She calls back in January 2021. Ari takes her back instantly. They resume the pattern — daily calls, virtual intimacy, promises of "soon." He flies to India again. Seven days. They cook eggs in a kitchenette and fight about the AC and it feels more like marriage than anything in his actual house. On the last day, he almost doesn't board the plane home.

But distance corrodes trust. A man appears in Meera's Instagram story — her gym trainer. Ari spirals into jealousy he can't justify and control he has no right to exercise. He's becoming the thing he hates: possessive, suspicious, monitoring her online status like a detective. He tries to rekindle his marriage. Takes Priya to dinner. She asks why he's trying, and the honest answer — *because I'm terrified of what I've become with Meera* — stays locked behind his teeth.

Everything breaks. Meera ends it: "This is too toxic." Truths Ari buried surface — she'd been talking to other men. He screams at her through a voice note, the cruelest words he's ever said. That night, Meera walks to her bathroom, opens the medicine cabinet, and swallows a fistful of paracetamol. Not to die. To make the noise stop. She vomits on the bathroom floor while her daughter sleeps in the next room. She texts him: "It's done. I'm home. I'm okay." He stares at his phone and understands, for the first time, that love and destruction can share a body.

Meera leaves Vikram — not for Ari, but for herself. She takes her daughter and moves to her parents' house. She begins studying for a postgraduate degree. She is, finally, free. But she doesn't come to Ari. Freedom, it turns out, doesn't point toward the man who loved you most. It points toward the woman you never got to become.

Priya leaves too — or rather, they both finally say the words. One chapter is told entirely from Priya's perspective: the wife who knew. Not the details, but the shape of the absence. The cologne for "work trips." The whispering on the couch after midnight. She's not angry. She's been mourning a living husband for five years. Her final words to Ari: "That's all I ever wanted. For you to stop pretending."

Ari is alone for the first time in his adult life. He learns the difference between lonely silence and solitary silence — one is a prison, the other a monastery. He turns forty in December. Meera texts "Happy birthday" at midnight her time. Priya texts nothing. He writes in his journal: *The man I was at thirty-two would not recognize the man I am at forty. And that's the whole point.*

In the final chapter, set in spring 2027, Ari still talks to Meera. Not daily. Not desperately. The way two people talk who've been through a war together and come out standing on opposite sides of an ocean. He loves her — from abundance, not scarcity. He doesn't need her to see him anymore. He sees himself. The phone sits on the counter, no longer the most important relationship in his life. He is, finally, the King — not because anyone crowned him, but because he learned to sit on the throne alone.

---

*Told through TikTok analytics, WhatsApp messages, voice notes, search histories, and journal entries, THE WOMAN IN MY PHONE is a novel about what happens when a device designed to keep everything temporary becomes the site of the most permanent feeling two people have ever known.*
