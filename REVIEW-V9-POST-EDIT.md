## 1. Ch 19 paternity-realization scene — Does the calculator-app sequence land as event?

**YES.** This is a real scene now, not a journal-entry summary.

The sequence works because of its physical precision: opens calculator → does the math he'd avoided → closes calculator → opens notes → finds the flight dates → the math had never been mine. Then the body takes over: phone face-down on the counter, notices the dish soap streak, sits on the kitchen floor, the flat clinical voice — *you knew. you knew. you knew.*

Two things make it land:

1. **The forehead deconstruction.** "A baby has the forehead of any human being who has a forehead. A baby's forehead is a forehead." That's Ari's voice at its best — self-lacerating clarity arriving as a simple declarative that unmakes four years of self-delusion in one sentence.

2. **The physical endpoint.** He sits on the floor not for dramatic reasons — "The floor was where I ended up." The hour of not-crying, waiting to cry, the crying not coming. That's specific and true. It resists the impulse to give Ari a big emotional release at the moment of revelation. The revelation is flat and terrible, which is how these things actually feel.

The journal entry after ("The baby isn't mine. She was sleeping with him.") now reads correctly as morning-after distillation, not the reveal itself. Proper sequence: event → processing → summary.

## 2. Ch 22 opening (May–July 2023) — Does the wrist-grab feel earned?

**YES. The bridge is doing the structural work it needed to do.**

The screen time dashboards (11 → 13 → 14 hours daily, average sleep 4h 13min) tell the deterioration story through data before the prose does. That's the novel's signature move — let the artifact carry the weight, then let Ari narrate underneath it.

Three specific beats that earn the wrist-grab:

1. **The cereal bowl.** The daughter pouring cereal for herself because nobody made breakfast. Ari saying "yes" in the wrong language. Her *okay* voice. This is the domestic-consequences beat the chapter needed — it shows the deterioration isn't just internal, it's radiating outward.

2. **The small fights escalation.** Recycling → dishwasher → school forms. Each one eighteen seconds or less. Each one a proxy for the real question (*why am I dying in here*). The fights aren't dramatic — they're banal, which is worse. By July he's picking them. The reader watches the pressure build.

3. **The Sameer text.** "Da call me when you can" — read, no reply. One beat. Shows Ari cutting off even the safety valve (which makes Ch 30 land harder later when Sameer shows up anyway).

By the time we hit "It happened on a Saturday," the reader has watched two months of a man at 14 hours screen time and 4 hours sleep, whose daughter is self-parenting and whose wife has perfected leaving rooms. The wrist-grab arrives as the inevitable crack in a pressurized system, not a plot device dropped in to move the story forward.
