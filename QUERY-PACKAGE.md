## QUERY LETTER — PERSONALIZED VERSIONS

### Version A: Annie Hwang (APL)

Dear Annie,

I'm querying you because your hunt for voice-driven literary fiction that stretches genre to new heights describes exactly what I've written — a novel told entirely through the artifacts of a digital life.

THE WOMAN IN MY PHONE is a literary novel complete at 57,000 words. Told through TikTok analytics, WhatsApp messages, voice notes, and search histories, it's *Normal People* for the age of infinite scroll.

Ari Velmurugan is a thirty-two-year-old Tamil American in Seattle with a dead marriage and a TikTok following that loves him more than his wife does. When a woman named Meera starts winning his Live song games every night from India, a DM becomes a four-hour call, then a five-year affair conducted across continents and screens. She's trapped in a marriage where her husband rapes her. He's invisible in a silent one. The phone is the only room where they both exist.

Over seven years, they meet twice. She gets pregnant — maybe his. She ghosts him for a year. He flies across the world and watches from a taxi as she walks back to the man who hurts her. She swallows pills on a bathroom floor. And through it all, two married people try to build something real inside a device designed to keep everything temporary.

Told in alternating perspectives — Ari's raw first person, Meera's intimate third, and one devastating chapter from the wife who knew — the novel explores desire, distance, and what happens when a phone becomes the most honest relationship in your life.

I am a first-generation Indian American writing from lived experience. By day, I lead product at a Fortune 500 company. This is my debut novel.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

A. Rathna

## SYNOPSIS (1 page, ~600 words)

ARI VELMURUGAN, a thirty-two-year-old Tamil American tech professional in Seattle, lives in a dead marriage to PRIYA — three feet away in the same bed, a thousand miles away in every way that matters. In July 2018, a woman named MEERA starts winning his TikTok Live song games every night. Within ten days, they've crossed every boundary two married strangers can cross through a screen.

What Ari doesn't know yet: Meera's husband VIKRAM rapes her. The phone isn't her escape. It's her only room.

Their affair falls into a rhythm of 137-hour call months and parallel cages — his silent, hers violent. Ari flies to India. Three days in a hotel room where they condense a lifetime into seventy-two hours. On the last morning, they have sex without protection. He watches from a taxi as she walks back toward the man who hurts her.

Meera discovers she's pregnant. "It could be yours," she tells Ari. For three years he lives inside that *could*. It's not his — the timeline never worked — but the ambiguity becomes its own kind of parenthood.

Then Meera goes silent. Thirteen months. No calls, no texts. Ari unravels. From Meera's side, the silence wasn't abandonment — she was holding her newborn daughter, choosing motherhood over the only man who ever made her feel alive.

She calls back. They resume the pattern — daily calls, virtual intimacy, promises of "soon." He flies to India again. Seven days that feel more like marriage than anything in his actual house. But distance corrodes trust. A man appears in Meera's Instagram. Ari spirals into jealousy he can't justify and control he has no right to exercise.

Everything breaks. Meera ends it. Truths surface — she'd been talking to other men. Ari screams at her in a voice note, the cruelest words he's ever said. That night, Meera walks to her bathroom and swallows a fistful of paracetamol. Not to die. To make the noise stop.

Meera leaves Vikram — not for Ari, but for herself. She takes her daughter, moves to her parents' house, begins studying for a postgraduate degree. Freedom doesn't point toward the man who loved her most. It points toward the woman she never got to become.

Priya leaves too. One chapter is told from her perspective: the wife who knew. Not the details, but the shape of the absence. Her final words: "That's all I ever wanted. For you to stop pretending."

Ari is alone for the first time in his adult life. He learns the difference between lonely silence and solitary silence. He turns forty. Meera texts "Happy birthday" at midnight. He still talks to her — not daily, not desperately. The way two people talk who've been through a war and come out standing on opposite sides of an ocean. He loves her from abundance, not scarcity. The phone sits on the counter, no longer the most important relationship in his life.
